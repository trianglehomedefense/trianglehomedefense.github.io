Bat work needs careful timing and documentation.

Bats require a measured approach: identify likely access, understand seasonal limits, and avoid unsafe or improper exclusion.

North Carolina-aware

Inspection before action.

Bat activity around vents, gables, roof returns, trim gaps, chimneys, or attic spaces should be evaluated carefully. The right plan depends on visible evidence, species behavior, timing, occupancy concerns, and applicable rules.

  1. Locate likely entry pointsWe inspect accessible exterior and interior indicators such as staining, droppings, rub marks, sound reports, and structural gaps.
  2. Document timing and restrictionsBat work may depend on season, occupancy signs, safety, and legal considerations.
  3. Plan exclusion and correctionWe explain what can be sealed, what should wait, and what needs further access or monitoring.

Measured, not rushed

Bat exclusion is prevention work.

The objective is to protect the home while avoiding unsafe action. That means documentation, timing, and structure-aware correction.

Entry points

Small gaps around trim, vents, returns, ridge areas, gables, and utility penetrations can allow activity.

Evidence

Staining, droppings, sound reports, flight observations, and homeowner photos help establish the next step.

Timing

Recommendations depend on what is observed, seasonal considerations, access, and safety constraints.

What to send

Start with visible details.

Photos of staining, droppings, vents, roofline gaps, attic indicators, or the area where bats are seen can make the first conversation more useful.

Emergency note: If a person or pet has had direct contact with a bat, or if a bat was found in a living space where people were sleeping, contact local health or animal control authorities promptly.
